Trigonometry Quiz

1. Prove: \((1 - \cos^2 A) \cdot \sec^2 B + \tan^2 B(1 - \sin^2 A) = \sin^2 A + \tan^2 B\)

2. If \(3 \tan \theta = \sec \theta\), then find the value of \(\cot \theta\).

3. A circus artist is climbing a 20 m long rope, which is tightly stretched and tied from the top of a vertical pole to the ground. Find the height of the pole, if the angle made by the rope with the ground level is \(30^\circ\).

4. An aeroplane when flying at a height of 4000 m from the ground passes vertically above another aeroplane at an instant when the angles of elevation of the two planes from the same point on the ground are \(60^\circ\) and \(45^\circ\) respectively. Find the vertical distance between the aeroplanes at that instant.

Question 1

Prove: \((1 - \cos^2 A) \cdot \sec^2 B + \tan^2 B(1 - \sin^2 A) = \sin^2 A + \tan^2 B\)

Solution: Since \(1 - \cos^2 A = \sin^2 A\) and \(1 - \sin^2 A = \cos^2 A\), substituting these into the equation simplifies both sides to \(\sin^2 A + \tan^2 B\), proving the identity.

Question 2

If \(3 \tan \theta = \sec \theta\), then find the value of \(\cot \theta\).

Solution: Dividing both sides by \(\cos \theta\), we get \(3 \sin \theta = 1\), so \(\sin \theta = \frac{1}{3}\) and \(\cos \theta = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2 \theta} = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}\). Thus, \(\tan \theta = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2}}\) and \(\cot \theta = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{1} = 2\sqrt{2}\).

Question 3

A circus artist is climbing a 20 m long rope, which is tightly stretched and tied from the top of a vertical pole to the ground. Find the height of the pole, if the angle made by the rope with the ground level is \(30^\circ\).

Solution: The height of the pole is given by \(h = 20 \times \sin 30^\circ = 20 \times \frac{1}{2} = 10\) meters.

Question 4

An aeroplane when flying at a height of 4000 m from the ground passes vertically above another aeroplane at an instant when the angles of elevation of the two planes from the same point on the ground are \(60^\circ\) and \(45^\circ\) respectively. Find the vertical distance between the aeroplanes at that instant.

Solution: Let the height of the lower plane be \(h\). Using \(h = d \tan 45^\circ\), we get \(h = d\). For the higher plane, \(H = d \tan 60^\circ = d\sqrt{3}\). Given \(H = 4000\), solving for \(d\) and substituting, we get the vertical distance \(4000 - \frac{4000}{\sqrt{3}}\).
